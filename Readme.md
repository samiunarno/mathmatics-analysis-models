**2026 Changchun University of Science & Technology — Mathematical Modeling Competition, Problem B**

## What this project is about

Companies have been laying off employees at an unprecedented rate over the last few years — tech giants, automotive companies, real estate firms, you name it. The question this competition asks is: *can we predict which companies are likely to lay off their workforce, and what features drive that decision?*

We were given data on 9,800 companies (training set) and asked to build a model that predicts layoffs for 200 unseen companies (test set). The data includes things like the industry, country, funding amount, employee count, revenue growth rate, and valuation.

---

| File | Description |
|------|-------------|
| `train.xlsx` | 9,800 companies with known layoff outcomes |
| `test.xlsx` | 200 companies — we predict these |
| `test_predictions.xlsx` | Our model's output (generated by running the script) |

**Columns in both files:**

| Column | Type | Description |

## Competition Questions & Our Approach

### Q1 — Exploratory Data Analysis

The first task was to dig into the data and find patterns that separate companies that laid off workers from those that didn't.

**What we found:**

- The overall layoff rate in the training data is **31.83%** — so roughly 1 in 3 companies experienced layoffs.
- `growth_rate` stood out as the only statistically significant numeric predictor (Mann-Whitney U test, p < 0.001). Companies with lower growth rates are much more likely to lay off workers.
- `funding_amount`, `employee_count`, and `valuation` showed no statistically significant difference between the two groups (p > 0.05).
- Certain industries and countries have notably higher layoff rates — visible in the `q1_industry_country.png` chart.

**Output figures:**
- `q1_target_dist.png` — shows how balanced/imbalanced the dataset is
- `q1_numeric_dist.png` — histograms and boxplots comparing layoff vs no-layoff companies across all numeric features
- `q1_industry_country.png` — which industries and countries have the highest layoff rates
- `q1_correlation.png` — correlation heatmap between all features

### Q2 — Building the Prediction Model

We tested four classification algorithms and compared them using 5-fold cross-validated AUC (area under ROC curve):

| Model | Validation AUC | Cross-Val AUC | F1 Score |
|-------|---------------|----------------|----------|
| Logistic Regression | 0.6198 | 0.6403 | 0.4724 |
| **Random Forest** | **0.6487** | **0.6732** | **0.4553** |
| Gradient Boosting | 0.6434 | 0.6614 | 0.3872 |
| Extra Trees | 0.6178 | 0.6459 | 0.3536 |

**Random Forest came out on top** with an AUC of 0.6487. Not a perfect model, but the data genuinely has limited discriminative signal — `growth_rate` carries most of the weight (45.2% feature importance), while the other features contribute much less.

**Feature importance ranking (from Random Forest):**

| Rank | Feature | Importance |
|------|---------|------------|
| 1 | growth_rate | 45.2% |
| 2 | employee_count | 14.3% |
| 3 | funding_amount | 12.0% |
| 4 | valuation | 12.0% |
| 5 | industry | 11.1% |
| 6 | country | 5.4% |

We also ran a **threshold sensitivity analysis** to see how precision/recall trade off at different decision cutoffs. The default 0.5 threshold gives the best balance for this dataset.

**Output figures:**
- `q2_roc_cm.png` — ROC curves for all four models + confusion matrix for the best one
- `q2_feature_importance.png` — which features matter and by how much
- `q2_sensitivity.png` — precision, recall, F1 across different probability thresholds
- `q2_learning_curve.png` — checks whether the model is overfitting or underfitting

### Q4 — Deep Analysis & Career Survival Guide

This section goes deeper into how features interact with each other and translates the model's findings into practical career advice.

**Key statistical finding from grouped analysis:**

Companies that *did* lay off employees had an average growth rate of **53.2%** — compared to **84.0%** for those that didn't. That's a 37% gap, which is the clearest signal in the entire dataset.

**Data-driven career survival tips:**
1. Work in stable industries (Healthcare, Finance) — historically lower layoff rates
2. Prefer companies with moderate employee counts (not too large, not too small)
3. Strong funding (> $1B) correlates with lower layoff risk
4. Company valuation alone isn't a reliable indicator — growth rate matters far more
5. Avoid companies with declining or stagnant revenue growth (< 50%)
6. Companies with global presence tend to weather downturns better

**Output figures:**
- `q4_pairplot.png` — scatter matrix showing how pairs of features relate to layoff outcomes
- `q4_career_guide.png` — visual summary of the survival guide

## Key Takeaways

The most important thing this analysis shows is that **revenue growth rate is the dominant signal for layoff risk** — far more important than how big a company is, how much funding it has raised, or what country it operates in. A company growing fast is much more likely to retain its workforce even during economic downturns, while stagnant or contracting companies are the ones cutting headcount.

For anyone thinking about career risk, the practical implication is simple: pay attention to a company's growth trajectory, not just its size or brand name.
